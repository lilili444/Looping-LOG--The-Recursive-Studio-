In recent years, the rise of artificial intelligence (AI) has reshaped the media, entertainment, and cultural industries, bringing both innovation and disruption. Nowhere is this transformation more evident than in the short-form video ecosystem, especially on platforms like TikTok. These platforms, originally built for quick entertainment, have become powerful engines of content production, algorithmic distribution, and audience engagement. At the heart of this shift is the growing integration of AI—from recommendation systems to content creation tools—which has restructured how media is made and consumed. 

For many creators, this change is not merely technological but emotional. As AI becomes capable of writing scripts, generating images, composing music, and even creating entire influencer personas, human creatives find themselves caught in a paradox. They are encouraged—or even forced—to use AI tools to stay visible in an oversaturated market, while simultaneously witnessing these tools erode their own professional identities. Anxiety, burnout, and a sense of replacement are becoming commonplace among independent artists, writers, and performers. Many feel trapped in a race they cannot win, constantly adapting to shifting algorithms, content trends, and monetization schemes. This “creative precarity” is deepened by AI’s ability to mimic human styles without attribution, often trained on datasets built from copyrighted or unpaid human work. The result is a crisis not just of labor, but of authorship, dignity, and expression.
